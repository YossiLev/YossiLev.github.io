- How did We Loose the Photons?
It is common to assume that light can also be viewed as made of particles – Photons. While EM waves sometimes present themselves as a continuous entity, there were some experiments that suggested that light should be quantized, that is, regarded as being made out of discrete balks containing discrete values of energy. These include the interaction (absorption and emission) of EM fields with matter particles. So, how can we understand such processes without quantizing the EM fields?
We agree that the matter system (e.g. Hydrogen atom) has discrete states of stability. Each state has a definite value of energy, but also of angular momentum.
Here is our interpretation of the interaction process, and how physics reached to the false conclusion that light is made of quantized entities.
The interaction with light includes a transition of the matter system from one stable state into another. So the total change of energy \((\Delta E)\) and angular momentum \((\Delta L)\) of the system also has discrete values. For example, an electron changes state from values of \((E_1, L_1)\) into another state \((E_2, L_2)\). So the change in EM values (absorbed or emitted) is \((\Delta E, \Delta L)\). If the values that \(L\) can accept are discrete as follows, then we get that \(\Delta L\) is also quantized to, and the most popular \(\Delta L\) value is a single. So the light that is emitted from (or collected by) the atom has values of \((E=E_2–E_1, L=\hbar)\) due to conservation of energy and angular momentum.
