In particle physics, saying that the electron and the positron have "different parity" refers specifically to

their **intrinsic parity**, a fundamental quantum property related to how their physical states behave under space reflection (reversing all spatial coordinates) . 

A key consequence of the **Dirac equation** is that spin-1/2 fermions (like the electron) and their antifermions (like the positron) must have opposite intrinsic parities. By standard convention, the electron is assigned a parity of **+1** (even), while its antiparticle, the positron, is assigned **\-1** (odd). 

---

Key Concepts of Particle Parity 

-   **Eigenvalues of Reflection:** In quantum mechanics, intrinsic parity is a phase factor that arises as an eigenvalue of the parity operation (reflection about the origin). Because applying parity twice restores the original state, these values are always either **+1** or **\-1**.
-   **The Fermion Rule:** While bosons and their antiparticles share the same parity, fermions and their antiparticles **must** have opposite parities.
-   **Convention-Based Signs:** Because single leptons cannot be created or destroyed (due to lepton number conservation), we cannot experimentally distinguish the absolute sign of a single lepton's parity. Therefore, it is a convention to assign constituents of normal matter (electrons, protons, quarks) a parity of $+1$ and their antiparticles $-1$. 

Experimental Evidence: Positronium 

The fact that electrons and positrons have opposite parity was famously tested through experiments involving **positronium**, a bound state of an electron and a positron. 

-   **Zero Angular Momentum State:** When positronium is in its ground state ($L=0$), its total parity is the product of the intrinsic parities of the electron and positron.
-   **Experimental Confirmation:** If Dirac's theory—predicting opposite parities—is correct, the total parity of this system should be **\-1** ($+1\times -1$). Experiments measuring the polarization of photons emitted during positronium annihilation have confirmed this prediction, providing evidence for Dirac's theory.

While they differ in charge and parity, electrons and positrons share several identical properties due to being antiparticle pairs:
