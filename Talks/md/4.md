It is a classic point of confusion, but you've hit on the exact reason why the Bohr model—while revolutionary—was eventually replaced.

### The Shape of Bohr Orbitals

To answer your question directly: **Yes.** In the original Bohr model, all orbits are strictly **circular**.

Bohr envisioned electrons as particles moving in defined, "planetary" paths around the nucleus. Because these paths are circles with a set radius ( $r$ ), the electron is always moving at a tangent to that circle. This means:

*   **Non-zero Angular Momentum:** In Bohr's framework, the angular momentum ( $L$ ) is quantized according to the formula  $L=n\hbar$  (where  $n=1,2,3...$ ). Since  $n$  cannot be zero, the angular momentum can **never be zero**.
*   **The "Flat" Atom:** Because the orbits are circular and exist in a single plane, the Bohr model essentially describes a 2D atom rather than a 3D one.

* * *

### Why this became a problem

While the Bohr model worked beautifully for Hydrogen, it ran into two major walls that lead us toward Sommerfeld:

1.  **The "Zero" Problem:** Later quantum mechanics (the Schrödinger equation) proved that ground-state electrons (s-orbitals) actually have **zero** orbital angular momentum ( $l=0$ ). Bohr’s model couldn't account for a "non-orbiting" electron.
2.  **Fine Structure:** When scientists looked closely at the light emitted by atoms, they saw single spectral lines were actually several lines bunched together. Circular orbits weren't complex enough to explain this.

### Enter Sommerfeld

Arnold Sommerfeld realized that circles are just a special case of **ellipses**. He expanded the model to allow for elliptical orbits, which introduced a second quantum number to describe the "flatness" or eccentricity of the orbit.

### 2\. The Stern-Gerlach Experiment (Deflecting the Beam)

This is the "gold standard" for measuring angular momentum directly. Scientists fire a beam of silver atoms through a non-uniform magnetic field.

*   **What happens:** The magnetic field exerts a force on the electron's magnetic moment, "pushing" the atom up or down depending on its angular momentum.

*   **The Calculation:** By measuring the exact distance between these spots and the strength of the magnetic field, we can back-calculate the precise value of the magnetic moment.
