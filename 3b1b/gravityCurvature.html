The Curvature of Gravitation
Apples fall to the ground, and planets orbit around the sun. Isaac Newton explained both findings, saying that bodies with mass attract other bodies, causing them change their speed. The calculation of the attraction force between mass 1 and mass 2 is given by the formula . F , equals G Mass 1 times Mass 2, divided by square of the distance between the masses
\[F=\frac{GM_1M_2}{R^2}\]
After Albert Einstein introduced the theory of relativity, then he tried the generalize the theory to include accelerated motion. The result was a more general theory, to be later named "General Relativity". The theory gave a new explanation to both accelerations and also the attractive forces of gravity, and became the new gravity theory. It suggested that masses affect the "fabric of space-time" and bend (or curve or distort) it. In this space-time, the motion of all the stuff (massive objects but also light!) is distorted so they follow the curved paths in the curved space-time. A new formula gives the connection between "Energy-Momentum T", which is the new name for Mass, and the geometric distortion of space time, now called "Einstein Curvature G". Both G and T are defined at each point of space, and at each moment of time. The formula is ever so simple, but the simple shape is misleading. \[G=T\] In reality this equation is so complicated, that we could not solve it (except for some simple cases).
But still, we remain with all masses curve space-time, and then masses move in space-time along the curved paths that are the "straightest" curves available on the curved space-time. Only when there are no masses at all (\(T=0\) everywhere), do we get no distortion (\(G=0\)), and then we call it zero-curvature, or flat-space.
What is this curved space-time that makes gravity? How can we see it? This is the goal of this presentation. So let's start talking about curved spaces.
One dimensional curved shapes, or curved lines - It is easy to imagine a curved line, and to tell about a line if it is curved or not (not curved would give a straight line).
Two dimensional curved shapes, or curved surface - A curved surface is also something familiar. We can tell the difference between a curved surface and a flat one by looking. Straight lines on a curves space?
We can imagine drawing a straight line on a flat surface, but could there be a straight line on a curved surface? The best way to handle this is to stand on a point of the surface and decide on which direction you want to go. The make a tiny step in the direction you chose. Usually, you will find yourself not exactly on the surface but rather a bit away. So now it is time to make a correction step and fall to the closest point of the surface.
On a flat surface, this procedure give a path along a straight line. On a curved surface, the path is "the straightest line possible".

Some things that are special to straight lines on curved surfaces, like there could be more than a single straight line between the same two points, and parallel lines may meet. Such things do not depend on the view point so they still exist even if the we are viewing the surface from the top. From this view point the curvature is a bit obscured, but the straight lines continue to tell the tail of a curved surface.
This is important, because when we start talking about a curved three-dimensional space, we do not have the option to directly view the curvature, by just looking at it from a higher dimensional point. So tracking the paths of the straight lines is a valuable option.
Back to Gravitation - 4 Dimensions
While we live in a tree-dimensional world, understanding curvature on three-dimensional spaces is not enough. The curved object, that drives gravity, has four dimensions. The three dimensions of space and another single dimension of time. Every "point" in that space is made out of 4 numbers (coordinates). Three numbers describe the location of the "point", while an additional number marks the time. The "point" is no longer a position, but rather an "event".
